How Bootstrapping Works

1
Start with the shortest maturity

The 1-year bond has no intermediate coupons, so it behaves like a zero-coupon bond. We can directly solve for the 1-year zero rate:

z1 = (F + C) / P1 - 1

This gives us our first point on the zero curve.

2
Use known rates to find the next

For the 2-year bond, we know z1 from step 1. The bond price equation has only one unknown (z2):

P2 = C/(1+z1) + (F+C)/(1+z2)2

Solve for z2 -- the 2-year zero rate.

3
Repeat sequentially for each maturity

For the n-year bond, all zero rates z1 through zn-1 are already known. The bond pricing equation becomes:

Pn = Σi=1..n-1 C/(1+zi)i + (F+C)/(1+zn)n

Only zn is unknown, so we solve directly.

4
Compute discount factors

From each zero rate, we derive the discount factor -- the present value of $1 received at that maturity:

DF(t) = 1 / (1 + zt)t

Discount factors strictly decrease with maturity and form the basis for pricing any cash flow.

5
Extract forward rates

Forward rates are implied by the relationship between adjacent zero rates:

f(t-1, t) = [(1+zt)t / (1+zt-1)t-1] - 1

These represent the market's implied rates for future periods and are key inputs to derivatives pricing.

6
The zero curve is complete

The bootstrapped zero curve, discount factors, and forward rates provide a complete, arbitrage-free framework for pricing any fixed income instrument. The par curve can be reconstructed from the zero curve to verify consistency.

Key Insight

Each successive bond maturity reveals exactly one new zero rate, building the curve sequentially from short to long maturities. This "bootstrap" process ensures the resulting curve is consistent with observed market prices -- no arbitrage is possible between the bootstrapped zero rates and the input bond prices.
