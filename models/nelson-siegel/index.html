Component Analysis

β0 — Level (Long-term rate)

A constant that determines the asymptotic long-term yield. As maturity approaches infinity, the yield converges to β0. This represents the market's long-run expectation for interest rates and typically ranges from 2% to 8%.

β1 — Slope (Short-term component)

Controls the slope of the yield curve. A negative β1 creates a normal (upward-sloping) curve; a positive value creates an inverted curve. The loading factor (1 - e-τ/λ)/(τ/λ) starts at 1 for short maturities and decays to 0 for long maturities, so β1 primarily affects the short end.

β2 — Curvature (Medium-term hump)

Creates a hump (or trough) at medium maturities. Its loading factor starts at 0, peaks at maturity λ, then decays back to 0. A positive β2 creates a hump; negative creates a trough. This captures the "belly" of the curve and is associated with monetary policy expectations.

λ — Decay Factor

Controls how quickly the slope and curvature components decay with maturity. A larger λ means slower decay -- effects persist further along the curve. It also determines where the curvature component peaks (at maturity = λ). Typical values range from 1 to 5 years.

Parameters

β0 — Level 5.00%
Long-term asymptotic yield. The rate the curve approaches at infinite maturity.
β1 — Slope -2.00%
Short-term deviation from level. Negative = normal (upward); Positive = inverted.
β2 — Curvature 1.00%
Medium-term hump/trough. Positive = hump; Negative = trough.
λ — Decay 1.50
Speed of exponential decay. Larger = slower decay, effects extend further out the curve.

Key Insight

Three factors -- level, slope, and curvature -- explain more than 95% of yield curve variation. This remarkable parsimony makes Nelson-Siegel the standard model for central banks worldwide, including the ECB, BIS, and many national central banks.
